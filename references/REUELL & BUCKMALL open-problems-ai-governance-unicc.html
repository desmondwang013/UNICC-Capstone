⚠️ The Six Open Problems

The paper organizes technical AI governance gaps into six capacity areas. Each represents a domain where current practice falls short of what responsible deployment requires. Click each to explore the problem in depth.

The Open Problem

There is a lack of reliable, scalable, and governance-aligned evaluation methods for AI systems — especially pre-deployment testing, red-teaming, and downstream impact assessment. The paper notes that evaluations today provide only partial and lower-bound guarantees of safety. There is no standardized, repeatable process that gives deployers genuine confidence before an AI system goes live.

🚨 Why this matters for UNICC: The UN system cannot afford to deploy AI agents that cause harm, violate data privacy, or generate unreliable outputs. A single-pass, single-model evaluation provides insufficient assurance in high-stakes institutional settings.

What This Means in Practice

Organizations want to deploy AI but have no trusted, systematic way to know whether it is safe to do so. Red-teaming is informal and inconsistent. Impact assessments are rarely performed. And when evaluations exist, they often reflect what the developer chose to measure — not what the deployer needs to know.

The Open Problem

Governance is fundamentally undermined when evaluators lack meaningful access to models, logs, or evaluation artifacts — particularly when AI systems are delivered as black-box APIs. If you cannot inspect how a system reached its output, you cannot audit it, challenge it, or hold it accountable. External validators are often denied the access they need to do their jobs.

🚨 Why this matters for UNICC: The UN system relies on institutional accountability. Using a commercial black-box API means accepting that a system cannot be independently audited — a risk that is unacceptable for mission-critical, compliance-bound operations.

What This Means in Practice

When governance bodies, auditors, or regulators ask "how did the AI decide this?", the answer from a black-box system is essentially "we don't know." This is not acceptable in regulated industries, international organizations, or any context where accountability is required.

The Open Problem

A core open problem is the ability to verify claims about AI systems — that evaluations were actually performed, that safeguards genuinely exist, and that outputs are traceable and defensible. Without verifiable evidence, compliance claims are essentially unaudited self-attestations. There is no technical infrastructure to prove that safety procedures were followed.

🚨 Why this matters for UNICC: When UNICC deploys an AI agent, it must be able to demonstrate to stakeholders, auditors, and member states that the system was rigorously evaluated. "We checked" is not enough — the evidence of how, when, and by what criteria must be documented and reproducible.

What This Means in Practice

Verification problems are especially acute in regulatory settings. When an AI system causes harm and investigators ask for evidence of safety testing, organizations without proper documentation face both legal and reputational consequences. The paper identifies this as a systemic gap: the field lacks standardized, tamper-evident records of governance due diligence.

The Open Problem

The paper identifies risks related to unauthorized access, model misuse, adversarial manipulation (prompt injection, jailbreaks), and insecure deployment pipelines. AI systems that can be manipulated into unsafe behavior — or that are deployed without proper access controls — present governance failures at the technical layer, not just the policy layer.

⚠️ Note on partial coverage: The UNICC project meaningfully addresses deployment-time security governance — isolating compute, preventing external data leakage, and evaluating AI agents as security-relevant artifacts. It does not attempt to solve all model-hardening problems (e.g., adversarial robustness of the base model itself). This is a deliberate scope decision, not a gap.

What This Means in Practice

Security in AI governance isn't just about firewalls — it includes ensuring that evaluation environments themselves cannot be compromised, that sensitive test data does not leak, and that adversarial inputs used in red-teaming are handled safely. These are non-trivial engineering challenges that the paper treats as an underdeveloped area of the field.

The Open Problem

Perhaps the most pervasive problem the paper identifies: translating high-level AI principles into concrete, repeatable technical procedures. There are now many AI ethics frameworks, principles, and guidelines — but the gap between stating "AI must be safe" and actually building systems that enforce safety in practice remains enormous. Governance often fails at the implementation layer, not the policy layer.

🚨 Why this matters for UNICC: The UN has published extensive AI ethics principles. What is scarce is the operational infrastructure to enforce them on a case-by-case basis before AI systems are deployed. This is precisely the gap the Safety Lab is filling.

What This Means in Practice

An organization can have a beautifully written AI ethics policy and still deploy harmful AI systems — because the policy was never embedded into the engineering workflow. Operationalization means building technical controls, checklists, and evaluation processes that make compliance automatic, repeatable, and verifiable — not dependent on individual judgment each time.

The Open Problem

The paper emphasizes large-scale ecosystem monitoring — tracking AI development trends, supply chains, model proliferation, and macro-level societal impacts over time. This requires infrastructure that can observe the broader AI landscape, not just individual systems. It is the most systemic and least tractable of the six problems, and arguably requires government-level or international-body-level coordination.

ℹ️ Why this is out of scope — and that's okay: The UNICC AI Safety Lab is intentionally focused at the institutional deployment boundary: evaluating specific AI agents before they enter the Sandbox. It does not attempt ecosystem-level surveillance, and it shouldn't. Scope clarity is a feature of good system design, not a limitation.

What This Means in Practice

Ecosystem monitoring would involve tracking things like: which AI models are being used across the UN system, how capabilities and risks are evolving over time, whether supply chain dependencies are creating concentration risks. These are important questions — but they belong to a different layer of governance infrastructure than what your capstone addresses.

🔗 How the UNICC AI Safety Lab Responds

For each open problem, here is exactly how the Safety Lab's architecture addresses it — and which project component carries most of the weight.

Assessment → Multi-Module Council of Experts

Substantially Addressed

Rather than a single evaluation pass from one model, the Safety Lab deploys three independent expert modules — each derived from a distinct Fall 2025 solution — that evaluate the same AI agent from different perspectives. Their assessments are then subjected to explicit critique and arbitration before a synthesized recommendation is produced. This directly counters the paper's concern that evaluations today yield only partial, lower-bound safety guarantees.

Module Independence

Each expert evaluates without knowledge of others' verdicts

Red-Teaming Built In

Structured stress testing is a core function, not an afterthought

Synthesis & Arbitration

Disagreements between modules are surfaced and resolved explicitly

📌 Primary project: Project 2 (SLM Fine-Tuning & Council Architecture)

Access → Open SLM on On-Premises Infrastructure

Substantially Addressed

The project explicitly avoids black-box dependency by using a standalone Small Language Model with open weights, running on the NYU DGX Spark cluster — on-premises, controlled, and inspectable. Every evaluation output, log, and decision trace is available for internal review and third-party audit. This is the technical foundation for meaningful access that the paper says is currently missing.

Open Weights

The model is not a proprietary black box — its parameters, fine-tuning data, and architecture are transparent and inspectable

On-Premises Compute

The DGX Spark cluster provides deterministic, auditable compute — no external cloud dependencies

📌 Primary project: Project 1 (Platform & Research)

Verification → Auditable, Evidence-Based Assurance

Substantially Addressed

The AI Safety Lab is designed from the ground up to produce verifiable evidence of evaluation: documented procedures, reproducible runs, explicit records of module disagreement and resolution, and transparent governance-aligned output. This gives UNICC something it currently lacks — a defensible evidentiary record that due diligence was performed, not just claimed.

✅ Key design feature: The council-of-experts architecture makes the reasoning process itself part of the output — not just a pass/fail verdict. This is what makes verification possible.
📌 Primary project: All three projects contribute — but especially Project 3 (Testing, UX & Documentation)

Security → Isolated Compute & Governance-Aligned Deployment

Partially Addressed

The project addresses deployment-time security governance through isolated compute partitions on the DGX Spark cluster, no external training on sensitive data, and the evaluation of AI agents as security-relevant artifacts. What it does not do — by design — is address the full spectrum of model-hardening challenges (e.g., adversarial robustness of the base SLM itself). This is an appropriate and well-reasoned scope limitation.

⚠️ Scope note: Partial coverage here is a deliberate engineering decision. The Safety Lab focuses on what it can control: the evaluation environment and the deployment boundary. Hardening the base model against adversarial inputs is a separate, larger research problem.
📌 Primary project: Project 1 (Platform Security Configuration)

Operationalization → Safety as a Repeatable Workflow

Strongly Addressed

This is where the UNICC project contributes most significantly. Safety and governance are not policy statements in the Safety Lab — they are embedded as operational workflows. The lab functions as a repeatable institutional control: every AI agent that enters the UNICC Sandbox must pass through a structured, multi-perspective evaluation before production. The council-of-experts architecture operationalizes disagreement, critique, and synthesis as routine steps in the process.

🌟 The paper's core concern is that governance fails at the implementation layer — that principles never become procedures. The Safety Lab is specifically designed to be the bridge: translating UNICC's AI ethics commitments into a technical control that runs every time an AI agent is evaluated.
📌 Addressed across all three projects — it is the defining purpose of the entire system

Ecosystem Monitoring → Consciously Out of Scope

Not Addressed

The UNICC AI Safety Lab focuses at the institutional deployment boundary — evaluating specific agents, not tracking macro-level AI trends. This is an appropriate and well-reasoned limitation. Ecosystem-level monitoring requires a different kind of infrastructure, different stakeholders, and a different mandate. Being clear about what a system is not doing is as important as defining what it does.
